Every organization is navigating the same five failure modes — whether they know it or not. The Intelligence-Driven Growth Framework maps every TCD engagement to one of five problem jobs: knowing who you are, knowing where you're going, knowing what you know, scaling what you've built, and earning your own people.

The Organizing Idea
Problems Compound
An organization that can't align can't build shared intelligence. Without intelligence it reinvents what it already knows. Reinvention without adoption leaves systems on the shelf. The five problems are sequential — and all active simultaneously.
How To Use This
Find Your Problem
Read the sub-problems inside each problem. When one sounds like your organization, open it. Browse the modules. Heart the ones that match your situation. Your selections build a live engagement in real time.
The Model
Modules Are Modular
Each module stands alone and delivers a concrete outcome. Most engagements combine 4–8 modules across two or three problems. Some sequence naturally — none require each other. TCD helps configure the right set.

The Identity
Problem
Who you are before you can go anywhere
You can't align what you haven't defined. Identity is the foundation every other problem sits on — strategy, operating models, OKRs, GTM motions. Without a clear, shared answer to who you are and how you grow, every downstream system is built on assumption. Most organizations believe these questions are settled. The evidence — misaligned leadership, drifting strategy, unclear differentiation — says otherwise.

Problem 02
The Alignment
Problem
Where you're going and how everyone gets there
Strategy exists. Work is happening. But the connection between them is implicit — built on assumptions about shared understanding that evaporate under organizational complexity. Work drifts from strategy. Investment accumulates in things that don't compound. Leadership tells slightly different stories about where the organization is going. The alignment problem isn't a culture problem. It's a systems problem.

Problem 03
The Signal
Problem
What you know and how it compounds
Every team is running discovery. Almost none of it accumulates. What one team learns disappears at the end of the quarter because the operating model was never designed to make intelligence cumulative. The organization keeps paying for the same insight cycle after cycle and never gets smarter. The signal problem isn't about having more data — it's about designing the architecture that makes what you learn compound over time.

Problem 04
The Reinvention
Problem
What you've built and how it scales
Teams are solving the same problems from scratch — not because they're unaware of what's been built, but because what's been built was never designed to be reused. The reinvention problem is an architecture problem. Accumulated work sits in documents, presentations, and tribal knowledge — undiscoverable, non-composable, and invisible to the next team that needs it.

Problem 05
The Adoption
Problem
How you earn the organization
The system is designed. The rollout is planned. Six weeks later, nobody's using it. Not because the system is wrong — but because the organization was never designed into it. Mandated adoption is borrowed trust. It runs out the moment the mandate author leaves the room. The only durable adoption is earned — where practitioners use the system because it makes their work better, not because someone is watching.
